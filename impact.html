Workforce, Wages, and the Zone

Tens of thousands of workers—many from the Caribbean and Latin America—kept the project moving. Canal Zone policies created inequities in pay and housing that reflected broader hierarchies of the era. Progressive-Era efficiency coexisted with segregation and coercive control.

Politics of Canal Making

U.S. recognition of Panama (1903) and the Hay–Bunau-Varilla Treaty secured a Canal Zone under U.S. control. The project strengthened the modern presidency and showcased American capacity—but also raised questions about sovereignty and intervention.
