Historical Background

Early visions, French setbacks, and the American turn in a Progressive Era context.

The idea of a water passage across the isthmus of Panama dates to the 16th century. In the late 1800s, France attempted a sea-level canal under Ferdinand de Lesseps but ran into landslides, disease, and financial collapse. By 1903, the United States took over the project and, after supporting Panama’s independence, began construction on a lock canal in 1904.

This history unfolded during the Progressive Era, when governments and reformers promoted expertise, public health, and large civic works. The canal became a canvas for those priorities and a test of American power abroad.

“We are not building a canal for the present alone. We are building it for the ages.”

— attributed to Progressive Era commentary on the Canal

The pivot to a lock canal reflected engineering pragmatism. It also aligned with Progressive Era values: centralized administration, expert commissions, and data-driven problem solving.
