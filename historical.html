Historical Background

The dream of linking the Atlantic and Pacific through Panama dates to the 1500s, but only the twentieth century marshaled the technology, capital, and political will to finish the job.

In the late 19th century, French engineer Ferdinand de Lesseps — builder of the Suez Canal — led the first modern attempt in Panama. Tropical disease, landslides, and financing problems doomed the project.

In 1903, the United States under President Theodore Roosevelt took control after supporting Panamanian independence. A lock-based design replaced the French sea-level plan, and by August 1914 the Canal opened to traffic, fundamentally transforming global trade and foreshadowing a new era of American power.
