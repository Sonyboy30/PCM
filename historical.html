Why a Canal in Panama?

Since the 1500s, explorers imagined a water passage linking the Atlantic and Pacific. In the late 1800s, France attempted a sea-level cut modeled after Suez but collapsed under landslides, tropical disease, and financial scandal. In 1903, after Panama’s independence from Colombia, the United States secured canal rights and reframed the project around a lock-and-lake design that better fit the terrain.

Key Background Points

  • French effort (1880s) fails due to yellow fever, malaria, and slides.
  • U.S. involvement (1903–1914) aligns with Progressive Era reforms and state capacity.
  • Lock canal plan reduces excavation and tames the Chagres River via Gatún Lake.
