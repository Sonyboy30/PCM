Historical Background

How an old idea became a Progressive Era project: from early visions and French failure to an American lock canal in 1914.

Early Visions and the French Attempt

The idea to link the Atlantic and Pacific by water dates to the 1500s. In the 1880s, Ferdinand de Lesseps attempted a sea-level canal in Panama. Landslides, disease, and funding problems ended the effort.

Progressive Era Context

As the United States embraced reform and efficiency, leaders backed a canal to speed trade, project influence, and apply modern management and public health methods.

From Plan to Lock Canal

American engineers adopted a lock design to handle the terrain. This choice reflected Progressive Era preferences for technical expertise and pragmatic problem solving.
